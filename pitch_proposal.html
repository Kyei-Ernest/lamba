Pitch Proposal

Borla Tracker

Ghana's Digital Waste Collection & Recycling Marketplace

Executive Summary

The Big Picture

Borla Tracker is a software-only digital marketplace that connects households who need waste pickup with independent waste collectors (Aboboyaa tricycle riders) and formal waste management companies (WMCs). Think of it as Uber for waste collection — we own no trucks, no bins, and no landfills. We simply provide the technology platform that matches demand with supply, processes payments digitally via Mobile Money, and unlocks the hidden value in Ghana's recyclable waste stream.

We are a team of university students in Ghana who understand the waste crisis firsthand — because we live in it. We are building a solution rooted in local realities, not imported assumptions.

01

Addressing the Market Need

Ghana's urban areas generate over 12,000 tonnes of solid waste daily, yet collection infrastructure consistently fails to keep up. The consequences are visible on every street corner.

The Core Problems

Problem Who It Hurts Scale
Unreliable scheduled pickups — Formal WMCs often delay collections by weeks, leaving bins overflowing. Households, businesses 40%+ of Accra residents report irregular collection
Inaccessible neighborhoods — Large compactor trucks cannot enter narrow, unpaved streets in high-density suburbs. Low-income communities (Nima, Ashaiman, Chorkor, etc.) Millions of urban residents in unserved alleys
Expensive tipping fees — Independent tricycle riders pay GHS 10–30 per trip at landfills, cutting deeply into slim daily earnings. Aboboyaa riders (informal collectors) Thousands of riders across Greater Accra alone
Illegal dumping & flooding — To avoid tipping fees, some collectors dump waste in open drains, causing cholera and severe seasonal flooding. Everyone — public health crisis Ghana loses millions of Cedis annually to flood damage
Wasted recyclables — Millions of plastic sachets ("pure water rubbers"), PET bottles, and scrap metals are discarded daily despite active demand from recycling plants. The environment, the economy Recycling plants operate well below capacity

Why Now?

  • MTN MoMo penetration is at an all-time high (40M+ registered accounts in Ghana), making digital payments feasible even in informal markets.
  • Smartphone adoption is growing rapidly in urban areas, while USSD (*800#) remains universally accessible on basic feature phones.
  • Government pressure is mounting — the Ministry of Sanitation is actively seeking private-sector digital solutions, and municipal assemblies are under public pressure to solve the "filth" crisis.

Operational Excellence

How It Actually Works

┌───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐ │ BORLA TRACKER PLATFORM │ │ (Pure Software Middleware) │ ├───────────────┬───────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────┤ │ CLIENT │ THE PLATFORM │ COLLECTOR / WMC │ │ │ │ │ │ App or USSD │ Matching Engine │ App, USSD, or IVR Voice Call │ │ → Select size │ PostGIS Zone Pricing │ → Accept job │ │ → Pay via │ Escrow Wallet │ → Collect waste │ │ MoMo │ PIN Verification │ → Enter client's 4-digit PIN │ │ → Get PIN │ Instant Payout │ → Get paid instantly to wallet │ └───────────────┴───────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────┘

Key Operational Principles

  1. We never touch the waste. We are a technology company, not a sanitation company. Collectors handle the physical work; we handle the digital coordination, payments, and verification.
  2. Dual-channel access. Smartphone users get a full-featured app with GPS tracking and map pinning. Feature-phone users (including illiterate collectors) use a toll-free USSD code (*800#) with local language IVR voice prompts (Twi, Ga, Hausa).
  3. PIN-based verification replaces expensive hardware. When a client requests a pickup, they receive a 4-digit SMS code. The collector enters this code via USSD after picking up the waste. No GPS sensors, no QR scanners, no barcode printers needed.
  4. Instant MoMo payouts. Collectors are paid directly to their MTN/Telecel MoMo wallets the moment a job is verified. No weekly bank transfers, no cash arguments at the gate.

The Reverse Marketplace ("Condemn" Module)

Beyond standard waste collection, we also run a reverse marketplace for recyclable materials:

  • Households with scrap metals, sachet rubbers (LDPE), plastic bottles (PET), or e-waste can list them for sale.
  • Specialized scrap collectors ("Koko-men") accept the job, weigh the materials on-site, and pay the household instantly via wallet transfer.
  • The collector then sells in bulk to registered recycling factories (e.g., Nelplast, Coliba, Tema Steel Works) at a higher wholesale price, keeping the profit margin.
  • The platform earns a small transaction fee on the wholesale payout.

Marketing & Go-To-Market Strategy

As a student team with limited capital, we cannot afford mass-market TV campaigns. Instead, we deploy high-impact, low-cost grassroots tactics:

Phase 1 · Months 1–3

Single-Neighborhood Pilot

  • Target: One well-defined suburb (e.g., Madina, Ashaiman, or our own university neighborhood).
  • Supply Side: Walk directly to the local Aboboyaa depot. Onboard 10–15 tricycle riders personally. Demonstrate USSD interface and instant MoMo payouts.
  • Demand Side: Go door-to-door. Register 100–200 households. Offer the first 3 pickups at a discounted rate to build trust.
  • Cost: Essentially zero — our own time and transport.
Phase 2 · Months 3–6

Word-of-Mouth & Local Radio

  • Collector Referrals: GHS 5 wallet bonus for every new collector onboarded via referral. Riders talk to each other daily at fuel stations — word spreads fast.
  • FM Radio Spots: Short jingles on Twi-language stations (Peace FM, Adom FM). A 30-second spot costs GHS 50–200.
  • Community Durbars & Church Announcements: Present at local town halls and through church/mosque networks — the most trusted information channels in Ghanaian neighborhoods.
Phase 3 · Months 6–12

WMC Partnerships & Multi-City Expansion

  • Approach mid-tier WMCs and offer our platform to manage their last-mile subcontractors. They get route tracking and compliance dashboards; we get access to their customer base.
  • Expand to Kumasi (second-largest city) using the same grassroots playbook.

Future Roadmap & Planned Features

Beyond the core waste collection and recyclables marketplace, we have identified additional community-driven features that strengthen the platform's social impact and long-term stickiness:

📸

A. Community Environmental Reporting ("Sanitation Watch")

  • Photo & Video Evidence: Any citizen can snap a photo or record a video of choked gutters, illegal dump sites, or overflowing public bins.
  • GPS-Tagged Reports: Reports are automatically tagged with location and routed to a public dashboard visible to the relevant Municipal Assembly (AMA, KMA, TMA).
  • Severity Scoring: Reports are categorized by severity — allowing authorities and volunteers to prioritize response.
  • Accountability Loop: Reported sites are tracked publicly. When cleaned, the resolver uploads "before and after" photos, closing the report.
🤝

B. Volunteer Cleanup Mobilization

  • When a reported site is severe but no municipal response is forthcoming, the platform broadcasts a volunteer cleanup call to registered users in the area.
  • Volunteers who participate earn "Green Points" (redeemable for data bundles, ECG credits, or collection discounts) and public recognition on a community leaderboard.
  • This turns passive reporting into active community-led intervention.
🚽

C. Sanitation Services Directory ("Toilet Pullers" & Septic Services)

  • A verified directory of licensed septic tank emptying and toilet-pulling services, listed by location, pricing, and user ratings.
  • Households can request a desludging service directly through the app or USSD, just like requesting a waste pickup.
  • This extends Borla Tracker from solid waste into full household sanitation — a natural product expansion.
